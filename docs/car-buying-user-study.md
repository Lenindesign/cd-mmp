# Car Buying User Study: C/D Marketplace App

## Objective

Understand how shoppers move from early vehicle research to a dealer action in this app, and identify which tools help them build confidence, affordability clarity, and deal confidence along the way.

## Core Journey

| Stage | User question | App tools today | What to learn |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Trigger and intent | What kind of car should I consider? | Search, browse vehicles, brand pages, rankings/body-style pages, 10Best modules, editorial reviews | Do shoppers know where to start when they have only a budget, body style, brand, or lifestyle need? |
| 2. Discover options | Which vehicles belong on my list? | Vehicle cards, filters, sort controls, rankings subnav, C/D rating, top-ranked modules | Do shoppers trust the ranking signals and understand why one vehicle is recommended over another? |
| 3. Evaluate shortlist | Which one fits my life best? | Vehicle detail page, quick specs, overview, trims, fuel economy, warranty, price history, comparison, saved vehicles | Which comparison signals matter most before a shopper is ready to price the vehicle? |
| 4. Affordability | Can I actually afford this? | Payment calculator, all-in-one budget-first calculator, taxes/fees, APR and term controls, trade-in estimate, lease-vs-buy AI | Do shoppers understand payment assumptions and how down payment, APR, term, trade-in, and incentives change the outcome? |
| 5. Deal confidence | Is this a good deal? | Deals hub, buying deals, lease deals, zero APR, cash-back, incentive modal, hero offer modules, negotiation advice | Can shoppers compare offers without feeling pressured or confused by incentive language? |
| 6. Dealer action | Where can I buy it and what happens next? | For Sale Near You, dealer locator map/list, live inventory, best deal/distance/price/rating sort, contact dealer, make offer | Do shoppers feel ready to contact a dealer, and do they understand what information they are sending? |
| 7. Return and continue | How do I keep track? | Account, saved vehicles, recently viewed, Google One Tap, email preview surfaces | Does saving/returning reduce repeated research or does the shopper restart each visit? |

## Participant Mix

- First-time new-car buyer: needs education and confidence.
- Budget-first shopper: starts with monthly payment or total budget.
- Used-car shopper: compares value, mileage, and price trust.
- Deal hunter: starts with incentives, APR, lease, or cash-back offers.
- Trade-in owner: needs current-car value before deciding.
- Brand/body-style loyalist: starts from Toyota, Honda, SUV, truck, sedan, or similar pages.

## Study Tasks

1. You need a small SUV and want to keep the monthly payment near $550. Find a vehicle you would consider.
2. Compare two vehicles and explain which one you would choose and why.
3. Find out whether changing down payment, APR, or term makes the vehicle more affordable.
4. Estimate your trade-in and apply it to the purchase decision.
5. Find the best available deal or incentive for your chosen vehicle.
6. Locate a dealer or available vehicle near you and decide whether you would contact the dealer.
7. Save or return to your shortlist so you can continue later.

## Metrics

- Time to first viable shortlist.
- Confidence score after each stage.
- Comprehension of C/D rating, rankings, and incentive terms.
- Calculator assumption comprehension.
- Payment confidence before and after using the calculator.
- Deal confidence before and after viewing incentives.
- Dealer-contact readiness.
- Drop-off or hesitation points.

## Hypotheses

- Shoppers will trust C/D rankings and reviews as a starting point, but still need clearer bridges from editorial confidence to affordability.
- Budget-first shoppers will move faster when the calculator recommends vehicles and deals, not only payment math.
- Trade-in and incentive context will materially change perceived affordability, but assumptions must stay visible.
- Dealer action will feel safer when live inventory, deal score, and contact expectations are clear before the lead form.

## Tool Map

| Need | Current tools |
| --- | --- |
| Find vehicles | `/vehicles`, `/rankings`, `/rankings/:bodyStyle`, brand pages, 10Best carousel |
| Understand expert POV | vehicle reviews, C/D rating, rankings, quick specs, overview, fuel economy, warranty |
| Compare options | `/compare`, vehicle ranking modules, trim selector, saved vehicles |
| Estimate affordability | `/auto-loan-calculator/light-steps`, payment calculator, lease-vs-buy AI, taxes/fees, APR, term, down payment |
| Include current car | trade-in value pages, trade-in prompt, trade-in estimate modal, VIN scanner surfaces |
| Find deals | `/deals`, buying deals, lease deals, zero APR, cash-back, incentive modal |
| Act locally | For Sale Near You, dealer locator map, live inventory, dealer sort, contact dealer, make offer |
| Continue later | account, saved vehicles, recently viewed, Google One Tap |

## Key Questions For The Study

- Where do users naturally start: rankings, search, deals, vehicle pages, or calculator?
- Which signals make users believe a vehicle is right for them?
- Where does the app ask users to make a decision before they feel ready?
- Which tools should be bundled into the same flow versus kept as separate expert tools?
- What information is missing before a shopper is willing to submit a dealer lead?

## Recommended Output

- A journey map of the seven stages above.
- A tool inventory grouped by shopper need.
- A friction/opportunity list ranked by impact.
- A recommended future funnel: Discover -> Shortlist -> Affordability -> Deal confidence -> Dealer action -> Saved follow-up.
